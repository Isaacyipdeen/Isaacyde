Sleep Deprivation

(PSYC121)

Effects of 42 Hr of Total Sleep Deprivation on Component Processes of Verbal Working Memory

Maintenance of information in an activated state over brief periods of time (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968). Not a one-dimensional construct. Baddeley's multicomponent model of working memory:
1) Central Executive:
-Direct attention to relevant information; suppresses irrelevant information
-Coordination of the slave systems

2) Phonological Loop:
-Consists of the articulatory loop and the acoustic store (or phonological store)
-Acoustic store with auditory memory traces that are subject to rapid decay
-Articulatory loop can revive the memory traces

3) Visuo-spatial sketchpad. Processes visual and spatial information

4) Episodic Buffer
-Acts as a buffer store between components of working memory, perception and long-term memory.
-Allows individuals to imagine new concepts
-Maintains a sense of time

Past Studies

Global measure of working memory:
Cannot be further specified in terms of the component processes that are affected
Cannot develop focused compensation strategies, or understand more about the underlying neurological systems

Tons of studies have showed that WM performance declines following 20 or more hrs of TSD. But several studies failed to prove such a correlation Why? A specific component process of WM may already be impacted but cannot be detected in a global score. E,g, Sustained attention is the cognitive process most consistently impaired by SD. But it is possible that other component processes of WM compensated for such deficits. Or if task did not require much attention.

This Study

Aims to find out how 42 hr of TSD will affect the component processes with regards to maintaining information in the WM in young healthy adults.
To do this, they came up with a computer-based WM test to measure 3 component processes of WM: attention, WM span, and efficiency of encoding into episodic memory

Hypothesis

All 3 components will not be equally affected; attention will be most affected, encoding least affected. Individual differences exist.

Procedure

Participants were recruited via advertisement in San Diego area
>18 women. 22 men. 19-39 years old
>Were asked to stop all alcohol and caffeine 3 days before their lab stay
>Maintained a regular sleep schedule one week before (sleep diaries and actigraph)
>Spent 6 experimental nights and 5 days, including 64hr of TSD in lab
>Can move about and do activities except strenuous exercise, exposure to sunlight, napping, consumption of stimulants. Monitored 24 hrs, administered cognitive tasks every 2 hours
>Took the verbal WM test while well rested and during TSD

Verbal WM Test

After subjects view a set of loading stimuli, the test alternates between test and study conditions
Subjects view a 3 secs presentation of a single, pronounceable five-letter nonsense word
During the test conditions, target is presented along with 3 similar nonsense words. Their task is to identify the nonsense word they have previously seen. They are not told how long they need to hold the information for. Pseudorandomized lags (0: item just studied, 4: item studied 4 trials before)
The computational model used estimated 3 parameters: Attention, displacement (probability that a new item displace old item in the WM); reciprocal of this represents WM span, and episodic encoding.

Results

Each of the parameters is being manipulated at different lags. The shape of the curve is very different, showing how TSD affects different component processes of WM differently, as hypothesized.
For lag 0 for all parameters, only the attention parameter when manipulated shows a decline in performance. Reducing DIS affects the performance the most after lag 0.
Note that these do not represent individual differences. Some people are greatly affected by TSD while some show improvements after TSD.

Limitations and Implications

This study only tested the verbal domain of WM. Results might differ for spatial or other types of tasks.
Secondly, different component processes of the WM is affected to a different degree across individuals.
There might be a possibility that participants change their strategies to account for the degradation, for example, only rehearsing a few words or encoding a few words.

More and more people faces sleep deprivation in their line of work. It is important to find individual’s strength and assign them tasks that engages the WM component processes that isn’t too severely affected by TSD.
Findings can also aid in future research when it comes to the neuropsychiatric population, such as schizophrenias, dementia and mood disorders, where deficits in WM memory is found.

References
